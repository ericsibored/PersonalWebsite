Endovascular Torque Device

Fall 2015-Spring 2016 [Written on 10-8-2016]

I am involved with a club at Pitt called Design Hub which works with physicians and university faculty to solve problems where there is an unmet need. In September of 2015, I applied and was accepted onto one of these design teams, to a pre-existing project called Endovascular Torque Device. During vascular surgery, the torque device is used to help guide a wire through the blood vessels of a patient. This is a delicate procedure which requires finesse to complete. The current torque devices in use are awkward to use for their small size, and require both hands to adjust mid-procedure, which is extremely inconvenient. As a result, two vascular surgeons from the University of Pittsburgh Medical Center requested that we work on improving the current design to support 1-handed use with a more user-friendly adjustment mechanism.

When I joined in the fall of 2015, many of the upperclassmen who led the project at it's conception had either graduated or moved on. Our team lead, Henry, recruited three sophomore bioengineers to help fill the gap and push the project to completion. We met weekly (and sometimes bi-weekly) throughout the semester to ideate, iterate and design possible torque devices that satisfied our given constraints, however interest and enthusiasm was curbed by the fact that we were initially unskilled in using Solidworks and properly modeling on CAD with design intent. Progress was slow, grueling work until we agreed to hold a mini "design-a-thon" to change things up a bit and hopefully generate some new ideas in a longer, more productive meeting.

The "design-a-thon" paid off and we were able to kick off the Spring semester with some viable options for how our prototype might work. Now that we were putting 3D printing back on the table, we recruited the help of a mechanical engineer for help and advice with refining our design and dealing with physical constraints (material properties, printing tolerances). With this, we quickly progressed into a series of small break throughs that led to a meeting with the physicians who had initially requested the design. In April of 2016, we brought them our prototype and our physicians were extremely pleased with the results. Extremely pleased is an understatement, and my words can't do it justice, but it was amazing to see our hard work pay off in the hands of a satisfied and happy doctor.
